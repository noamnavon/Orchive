Narrative

The Plot

The story unfolds with fifteen years of solitude on the moor, endured by the outcast after being ostracized from his fervently religious indextown for his lack of faith. Upon his return, he seeks out his lost love, Melinda. Their reunion is silent but profound, her recognition clear in the gleam of her eyes.


Seizing an opportune moment, he persuades her to flee with him, to begin anew.

In time, they make their escape. He has vowed to rescue her from her past, to offer her a fresh start.


As she recounts the events during his absence, he discovers she was wed to a man of faith, though doubts linger about her consent to this union. Together, they share a night.


Come morning, he finds himself alone. His search ends in horror when he discovers Melinda's lifeless body, victim to the village's vengeance for her perceived betrayal. The fifteen years of waiting have gone to waste, and with this incomprehensible notion, he gets insane. What he describes as a “beautiful pain” soon turns into a tantrum, and he starts killing anyone he sees. Fatigue overtakes him by nightfall; he collapses, and the church's followers apprehend him. Notably, in this sixth track, the music ceases abruptly, signaling his loss of consciousness.


Dawn of the final day presents a grim tableau—an execution. Confronted with the noose and executioner, the gathered crowd in their white garb embodies a hollow sanctity. In these final moments, the outcast harbors only hate and apathy, his last utterance a chilling promise: “I will come for you.” The crowd's discomfort is palpable; they carry out their duty not with zeal, but with a sense of weary obligation. This dissonance, this feigned morality, stands as a potent theme throughout the album—evil does not proclaim itself but masquerades behind acts justified as serving some grander good. Thus, the antagonist meets his end, hanged by a community cloaked in holiness. As his life ebbs away, so too does the music, both fading to silence.

Cynicism

'Still Life' is aptly titled, presenting a sharp dichotomy between societal virtues and the underlying malevolence it often masks. Through the eyes of the narrator being vilified by a pious society, Opeth critiques the hypocrisy inherent within. The album reflects this sentiment through:

  • 'The Moor,' with the oxymoronic lyric “Still calm with my mind at peace” amidst chaos.
  • 'Godhead's Lament,' ironically named given its focus on the sorrow of a coerced atheist.
  • In 'Benighted,' the album's most romantic song, the lyrics 'you are flawed as all of us' starkly contrast with the song's intimate tone. Additionally, the phrase 'Atone with your lonely soul' strikes a dissonant chord, as it contradicts the narrator's atheistic convictions.
  • 'Serenity Painted Death,' paradoxically named as it explores the 'beautiful pain' surrounding death.

Guilt

The album delves into the burden of guilt. A man, essentially good and yearning for simple love and peace, is demonized and exiled. It's suggested that he might have thrived in a secular environment, but the rigid dogma of his village condemns him. This external judgment is internalized, as the narrator sees himself as a defiler. These settings lead to self-destruction and madness, which in turn results in actual inhumane deeds.

Music

Sound

The sound of the album is rich and layered. Opeth deliberately avoided using digital instruments, quantization, harmonizers, or pitch correction tools. The principal list of instruments includes:

  • Electric Guitar
  • Acoustic Guitar
  • Bass Guitar
  • Drums
  • Vocals

Despite this seemingly minimalist setup, the sophisticated use of effects and layering positions this album as one of the most sonically rich in metal history.
It's worth paying special attention to the reverb, particularly on the snare drum, to appreciate its quality.

Genre

Broadly speaking, this album stands out as a formidable Death Metal work, aligning seamlessly with the narrative's intensity. Yet, delving into details reveals surprising combinations. Ambient passages contribute mood and atmosphere throughout the album. There's also an abundance of ballads and folk influences. The vocalist's range spans from gentle, melodious singing to soulful deep growls. However, the pervasive sense of darkness remains constant; there are no departures into lighter moods.
